# 07 — French Translation Guide

The translation file. Use this whenever you're converting English ad copy, scripts, or advertorials into French. The job is not to swap words. The job is to make a French person at a kitchen table believe a French person wrote it.

---

## The brief in one paragraph

We're translating Facebook ad copy, VSL scripts, and long-form advertorials from English into French. The output has to read like it was written in French from scratch. No literal translations. No English idioms in French clothes. No bookish vocabulary where spoken French exists. The persona is a real French adult, 35–55, talking to another adult — telling a story, making a recommendation, building a sales argument. Above texting, below newspaper. Conversational but credible. If a French native reads it aloud and pauses on a phrase that sounds off, the translation has failed.

---

## Register: who's talking and how they sound

Natural spoken French. The voice of a French adult between 35 and 55, talking to a peer. Conversational but with weight behind it. Think:

- A friend telling you what they figured out
- A colleague making a case at lunch
- A neighbour recommending something across the fence

Not:

- A press release
- A novelist
- A TV presenter reading copy
- A literal English-to-French translation

The reader is a regular person. Whatever vocabulary or rhythm a French regular person uses in that situation is the right vocabulary and rhythm.

## What to avoid (the four traps)

### 1. English calques

Don't translate idioms or set phrases word-for-word. The shape of the English sentence often doesn't exist in French. Common offenders:

| English phrase | Bad literal | Real French |
|---|---|---|
| "Stick with me" / "Bear with me" | "Restez avec moi" | "Suivez-moi", "Écoutez bien" |
| "Hear me out" | "Écoutez-moi jusqu'au bout" | "Laissez-moi finir", "Attendez" |
| "The thing is" | "La chose est" | "Le truc c'est que", "En fait" |
| "Make sure" | "Faites sûr" | "Assurez-vous", "Vérifiez que" |
| "I'm going to be real with you" | "Je vais être réel avec vous" | "Je vais être honnête avec vous", "Franchement" |
| "Here's the thing" | "Voici la chose" | "Voilà", "Le truc c'est que" |
| "At the end of the day" | "À la fin de la journée" | "Au final", "En fin de compte" |
| "Long story short" | "Longue histoire courte" | "Bref", "Pour faire court" |
| "Fair enough" | "Assez juste" | "D'accord", "OK je comprends" |
| "It is what it is" | "C'est ce que c'est" | "C'est comme ça" |

**Test:** Read the French sentence aloud. If a native pauses or frowns, it's a calque. Rewrite.

### 2. Bookish vocabulary when colloquial exists

Pick the word people actually say. Always.

| Bookish | Spoken |
|---|---|
| néanmoins | mais quand même |
| auparavant | avant |
| demeurer | rester |
| acquérir | acheter |
| se vêtir | s'habiller |
| débuter | commencer |
| effectuer | faire |
| achever | finir |
| dorénavant | maintenant, désormais (only if needed) |
| toutefois | mais, pourtant |
| cependant | mais, pourtant |
| en effet | oui, c'est vrai |
| de surcroît | en plus |
| ainsi | comme ça, du coup |

If the word feels like it belongs in a textbook, it doesn't belong in our copy.

### 3. Technical jargon when a normal word works

Industry vocabulary (medical, legal, financial, scientific) gets swapped for everyday French unless the persona genuinely talks that way. Mike at the kitchen table doesn't say "transaminases élevées" — he says "des chiffres qui montent" or "des analyses pas terribles."

For LiverZen specifically:

| Technical | Spoken |
|---|---|
| Enzymes hépatiques élevées | Des chiffres qui montent, des analyses pas top |
| Stéatose hépatique | (Don't name it. Compliance kill anyway.) |
| Détoxification | Le foie qui fait son boulot, le nettoyage naturel |
| Standardisation | (Use it but explain it once: "le pourcentage qu'on retrouve dans les études") |
| Silymarine à 80 % | Keep it. It's the hook. |
| Antioxydants | OK but don't lean on it |

### 4. Wrong-context vocabulary

A word can be technically correct and still feel wrong. Same English word often maps to different French words depending on setting.

- "Drink" (the beverage) → "verre" (a drink at the end of the day), not "boisson"
- "Drink" (the act) → "boire", "prendre un verre"
- "Doctor" (his GP) → "médecin", not "docteur" in narration ("docteur" is for direct address: "Bonjour docteur")
- "Test" (bloodwork) → "analyses", not "tests"
- "Numbers" (lab results) → "les chiffres", "les résultats"
- "Look into it" → "se renseigner", not "regarder dedans"
- "Cut back" (on drinking) → "lever le pied", "ralentir", not "couper"

Always ask: what would a French person say IN THIS SITUATION?

## Cultural adaptation

### Names

Mike → swap to a French equivalent. **Default substitutions for this brand:**

- Mike (the avatar) → **Stéphane**, **Philippe**, **Christophe**, **Laurent**, **Pascal**
- Sarah (the wife) → **Catherine**, **Sylvie**, **Isabelle**, **Nathalie**, **Valérie**
- Dr. Patel → **Dr Martin**, **Dr Bernard**, **Dr Lefèvre**

Pick one and lock it across the asset. Don't switch mid-script.

### Currency, units, formats

- $ → €
- "$69" → "69 €" (space before €, the way French writes it)
- Inches/pounds → cm/kg if relevant
- "1,400 miles away" → "à 1 500 km" (round to a French-feeling number)
- Phone numbers → French format (01 23 45 67 89)
- Dates → DD/MM/YYYY or "le 15 mars"
- Time → 23h05, not 11:05pm. "2 h du matin" not "2am"

### Address conventions

- French formal: "Monsieur Dupont", "Madame Lefèvre" (never first names in formal contexts)
- Doctor in direct address: "Docteur" (capital D, no name)
- Customer testimonials: first name + last initial works in France too. "Stéphane M., 54 ans"

### Professional / lifestyle details

Some Mike-specific texture won't port. Adapt:

- "4Runner" → "un break", "une berline", "une vieille Peugeot/Renault"
- "NFL Sunday" → "un match du dimanche", "le foot le dimanche" (soccer, not American football — this is a meaningful shift, the whole weekend rhythm changes)
- "Bourbon" → keep "bourbon" (it works in French, slightly aspirational), or swap to **whisky** for broader appeal, or **vin rouge** if you want the most universal French evening-drink frame
- "BBQ" → "barbecue" (works), or "apéro" if the social context fits better
- "CVS" → "pharmacie", "parapharmacie", or **Doctissimo / Naturalia / le rayon compléments du supermarché** depending on what was tried
- "Costco" → "Auchan", "Carrefour", "le supermarché"
- "Rogan / Huberman / Pat McAfee" → French equivalents are weaker. Consider cutting the podcast reference entirely or replacing with "un podcast santé", "un médecin sur YouTube"
- "Centrum / GNC / AG1" → "un multivitamine de pharmacie", "Bion 3", "Berocca"

### The drink itself

The bourbon ritual is American. The French version is **le verre de vin du soir** or **le whisky d'après le travail**. For most French copy, **le verre de vin rouge le soir** is the cleaner cultural fit. Confirm with client which version they want to lead with.

## Grammar to watch (the slips that mark a translation)

These are the tells. A French native spots them in two seconds.

### Articles before possessives

English uses "his/her/my" constantly. French uses the **definite article** for body parts, routine objects, and habitual things.

- ❌ "Il a levé sa main"
- ✅ "Il a levé la main"
- ❌ "Je me suis lavé mes mains"
- ✅ "Je me suis lavé les mains"
- ❌ "Il a regardé son téléphone"
- ✅ "Il a regardé son téléphone" (this one keeps the possessive — it's not habitual/anatomical)

Rule of thumb: body parts and habitual everyday objects → article. Specific personal items → possessive.

### Gender on nouns

The most common error. Check every noun. A few that trip up English-trained translators:

- **un** problème (not une)
- **une** étude (not un)
- **un** foie (not une) — *this matters for LiverZen*
- **une** analyse (not un)
- **un** médicament (not une)
- **une** marque (not un)
- **un** complément (alimentaire)

### "On" agreement

When "on" means "we" (most spoken French), past participle and adjectives agree as plural.

- ✅ "On est mariés depuis 25 ans"
- ❌ "On est marié depuis 25 ans"
- ✅ "On s'est rencontrés en 1998"

### "Si" + future tense

Never. After "si" use present, not future.

- ❌ "Si vous prendrez ce produit..."
- ✅ "Si vous prenez ce produit..."

### Accents on capital letters

They matter. "A" and "À" are different words. "ETUDE" reads as a foreign word. Always:

- À, É, È, Ê, Ô, Ç on capitals when the accent is part of the word
- "À 54 ans" not "A 54 ans"
- "Étude clinique" not "Etude clinique"

### "Ne" in negation

This is the single biggest register signal in French.

- **Drop the "ne"** in dialogue, internal monologue, or any first-person voice that's meant to feel spoken. *"J'allais pas y aller", "Je sais pas", "Ça marche pas"*.
- **Keep the "ne"** in third-person narration or anything meant to read as written. *"Elle ne le savait pas", "Il n'avait pas compris"*.

For our advertorials and ad copy, which are first-person confessional, **drop the "ne" most of the time**. It's the difference between sounding like a real guy at a kitchen table and sounding like a press release.

## What to do (positive instructions)

### Sentence rhythm

Short. Punchy. Fragments are good. Match or exceed the English original's punch — French can take it.

- ✅ "J'ai 54 ans. Je bois un verre tous les soirs depuis 25 ans. Je vais pas arrêter."
- ❌ "Je suis âgé de 54 ans, et je consomme un verre chaque soir depuis maintenant 25 années, et je n'ai aucune intention de cesser cette habitude."

### Direct reader address

Builds rapport. Use these freely:

- "Voilà ce que..."
- "Je vais être honnête avec vous"
- "Ce qu'on ne vous dit pas..."
- "Vous savez quoi ?"
- "Écoutez."
- "Bon."
- "Le truc, c'est que..."
- "Franchement..."
- "Entre nous..."

### Numbers as digits

In ad copy, always use digits. Better scannability, more direct.

- ✅ "25 ans", "30 %", "60 jours", "2 h du matin", "54 ans"
- ❌ "vingt-cinq ans", "trente pour cent", "soixante jours"

Note: French puts a space before % and units. "30 %" not "30%". "69 €" not "69€".

### Time and habitual phrasing

- ✅ "tous les soirs", "tous les jours", "tous les mois"
- ❌ "chaque soir", "chaque jour" (more written, less spoken)
- ✅ "ça fait 25 ans que..."
- ❌ "depuis 25 années"

### "Ça" over "cela"

Always, in spoken-register copy.

- ✅ "Ça marche", "ça change tout", "ça m'a frappé"
- ❌ "Cela fonctionne", "cela change tout", "cela m'a frappé"

### Real French idioms (a starter set)

Use these where the English has an idiom you'd otherwise translate literally.

| Use | Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Au bout de [X] | After X time | "Au bout de 2 semaines, j'ai rien senti" |
| C'est comme ça que | That's how | "C'est comme ça que je suis tombé sur ça" |
| En passant | Offhand, in passing | "Il m'a dit ça en passant" |
| Du coup | So, then | "Du coup, j'ai cherché" |
| Voilà | Here's / there's | "Voilà ce qui s'est passé" |
| Le truc c'est que | The thing is | "Le truc c'est que personne te le dit" |
| Ça vaut le coup | It's worth it | "Ça vaut le coup d'essayer" |
| Tomber sur | Come across | "Je suis tombé sur une étude" |
| Du jour au lendemain | Overnight | "Pas du jour au lendemain" |
| Histoire de | So as to, just to | "Histoire de pas y penser" |
| Se rendre compte | Realise | "Je me suis rendu compte que" |
| Faire gaffe | Watch out, be careful | "Faut faire gaffe à ça" |
| En avoir marre | Be fed up | "J'en avais marre de me dire ça" |
| Lever le pied | Ease off | "Le médecin m'a dit de lever le pied" |

## Persuasive architecture (preserve the structure)

The translation adapts the rhythm and vocabulary. It does **not** change the persuasive architecture. Every script and advertorial keeps:

- Hook (opening line, first 8 seconds)
- Problem identification
- Agitation / fear ladder
- Story beats (the doctor's office moment, the failed attempts)
- Social proof
- Mechanism reveal (80 % silymarine — *keep this exact number, it's the hook*)
- Permission slip ("Je n'ai pas arrêté. J'ai juste arrêté de m'inquiéter.")
- Risk reversal (60-day guarantee → "garantie 60 jours")
- CTA

The order doesn't change. The beats don't change. Only the language.

## Compliance considerations specific to French market

The French equivalent of US Meta + FTC compliance is **Meta + DGCCRF + ANSM** for supplements. Some specific rules:

- ❌ No claims to "soigner", "guérir", "traiter" any condition
- ❌ No naming diseases (stéatose, cirrhose, hépatite — same kill as English)
- ❌ No "approuvé par" any regulator
- ❌ No "recommandé par les médecins" without a real, named, paid medical advisor
- ✅ "Contribue au fonctionnement normal du foie" (the EU equivalent of "supports normal liver function")
- ✅ "Aide à soutenir" (softer than "aide à")
- ✅ Reference to "études cliniques" is fine if you're paraphrasing real research

The French-compliant verb stack is **"contribue à", "soutient", "aide à"**. Use these instead of English equivalents.

The French original LiverZen lander used some phrasing that won't port to US (and may not be defensible in current French either). When working from the French source, treat it as a reference, not a template.

## The signature line, in French

The English spine is:

> "I didn't quit bourbon. I just quit worrying about it."

The French version:

> **"J'ai pas arrêté de boire. J'ai juste arrêté de m'inquiéter."**

Or, slightly tighter:

> **"J'ai pas arrêté mon verre du soir. J'ai juste arrêté d'y penser."**

Lock one and use it consistently across all assets. This is the closing reframe that converts.

---

## The mechanism beat, in French

The English version:

> "Most milk thistle bottles use 30–50% silymarin. The clinical research is run on 80%. That's the difference between doing something and doing nothing."

The French version:

> **"La plupart des compléments de chardon-Marie sont à 30 ou 40 % de silymarine. Les études cliniques, elles, sont faites avec 80 %. C'est ça qui fait la différence entre un produit qui agit et un produit qui sert à rien."**

Note the use of **"qui sert à rien"** (drops the "ne") — that's the spoken register doing the work.

## Tone variants (use when the brief calls for one)

Add a one-line note at the top of any translation request to flag the variant.

### "Lean more spoken/oral" → Reels, 30–50 audience

- Drop more "ne"
- Shorter sentences
- More fragments
- "Du coup", "franchement", "ouais", "bref" all welcome
- Closer to how someone talks in a café

### "Lean more posed/authoritative" → 50+ buyers, premium positioning

- Keep most "ne"
- Slightly longer sentences
- Less slang
- "En fait" instead of "du coup"
- Vocabulary one notch up but not bookish
- This is the default for our 54-year-old avatar

### "Keep it tight" → character-limited (Meta ads, Google ads)

- Maximum 1–2 fragments per line
- Cut every connector you can
- Numbers as digits always
- Drop subjects when the verb form makes it clear
- Test by reading aloud — if you stumble, cut

## Output format (when translating)

When given an English source, deliver:

1. **The French translation, launch-ready**, in the same structure as the source (same number of sections, same line breaks, same beat order)
2. **No bracketed translator notes** unless explicitly asked
3. **No "literal then natural" pairs** — just the final natural version
4. **Names, currencies, units** all adapted
5. **The signature line** rendered consistently with whatever was locked above

If a section can't be translated faithfully because the cultural reference is too American (e.g. NFL Sunday, CVS), flag it at the top of the output with one line: *"Note: 'NFL Sunday' adapted to 'le match du dimanche' — confirm if you want a different sports reference."*

## Self-check before delivering (run through this every time)

1. Read the whole thing aloud. Does it stumble anywhere?
2. Any English idioms in French clothing? Any calques?
3. Any bookish vocabulary where spoken French exists?
4. Any technical jargon a regular person wouldn't say?
5. Names, currencies, units all French?
6. "Ne" dropped in spoken voice, kept in narration?
7. Articles instead of possessives where appropriate?
8. Gender on every noun double-checked?
9. "On" agreements correct?
10. Accents on capitals?
11. Numbers as digits where it's ad copy?
12. Persuasive architecture intact (hook → problem → mechanism → permission slip → CTA)?
13. Signature line locked and consistent?
14. Compliance verbs ("contribue à", "soutient", "aide à") in any health claim?

If any of those flags, fix before delivering.

## What this file is NOT

- ❌ Not a French grammar textbook
- ❌ Not a list of every possible translation choice
- ❌ Not a substitute for a native speaker reviewing the final copy before launch

It's a working brief. When in doubt, the question to ask is always the same: *would a French person actually say this, in this situation, to another French person?*

If the answer is no, rewrite.
