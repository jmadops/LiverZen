The thing about your liver is, it doesn't tell you when something's wrong.

I work hard.

I run my own roofing business. 60-plus hour weeks. On a roof at 6am, on the phone with a supplier at 8pm.

And I like a little bourbon at the end of the day.

Hell. I've earned it.

You know the feeling. It's 9pm, you've been on the go since 5. Finally me time. Two fingers of bourbon, one rock.

Bliss.

I'm not a hardcore drinker. I'm a guy who has a couple after work.

But I'm not stupid either

You hear the podcasts. The Hubermans, the Rogans. 14 units a week is the limit. Past that, you're doing damage you can't see.

I'd been past it. Most weeks. By a lot.

So last March I booked the physical I'd been putting off for three years.

I sat down across from my doctor and he said the thing I wasn't ready for.

"Your liver enzymes are creeping up. I suggest you take a few weeks off drinking to see where we're at."

I drove home knowing two things. I wasn't going to quit. And I needed another way.

What's actually happening in there

I asked him what "creeping up" actually meant.

He pointed at one number on the chart. ALT. An enzyme your liver releases when liver cells are under stress.

Then he scrolled back. Three years of numbers. Each one a little higher than the last.

"Your liver doesn't have pain receptors. None. So you can be 10 years into something and feel completely fine. The first symptom most people get is when the numbers stop creeping and start running."

He let that sit.

"Two drinks a night for 25 years isn't a binge. It's not what people picture when they think of liver trouble. But it's a slow constant load on an organ that doesn't complain. You don't feel anything because the liver's not built to make you feel anything. Not until it's late."

I'd been treating I feel fine as proof I was fine.

He'd just told me that's exactly backwards.

What he told me next

I asked him what I should actually do. If I wasn't quitting.

He looked at me like he was deciding whether to say the next part.

"Have you ever heard of milk thistle?"

I told him yeah. CVS. $14 bottle. Took it for two weeks, felt nothing, decided milk thistle was snake oil like every other supplement on the shelf.

He nodded like he'd heard this exact answer before.

"The bottles you find in most pharmacies aren't standardised the same way. The clinical research on milk thistle was almost all run on extract that's standardised to 80% silymarin. Silymarin is the active compound. Most over-the-counter bottles are 30 to 50%. Some of them aren't standardised at all."

He paused.

"I take the 80% version myself. I have for about four years."

I didn't say anything for a few seconds.

In nine years of seeing this man for annual physicals, this was the first time he'd told me what he personally did. Not what the literature recommends. Not what he tells his patients. What he does.

He didn't write it down. He didn't have a brand to recommend. He just answered a question I hadn't quite asked.

Why I almost didn't bother

I'd tried all the BS supplements before… it's snake oil!

I had a cabinet full of half-empty bottles already. Multivitamin. Fish oil. Glucosamine. The CVS milk thistle I'd written off three years earlier.

Here's what I thought about supplements. And I'd guess you think it too.

It's all snake oil.

Half of it is just expensive vitamins your body doesn't absorb.

But, I knew I'd rather try something than totally quit drinking.

So I went and looked at the CVS bottle I'd written off.

The label said milk thistle extract.

It didn't list a silymarin percentage anywhere.

I called CVS. The pharmacist looked it up. 30%.

I'd never actually tried milk thistle

I'd taken a third of the active concentration the research was run on, for less than two weeks, and concluded that "milk thistle doesn't work."

I hadn't tried milk thistle.

I'd never tried milk thistle.

I ordered the 80% version that night.

What I felt, what I didn't feel, and what showed up on the bloodwork.

Week 7: back for bloodwork

I went back for follow-up bloodwork.

My ALT was back to normal.

My doctor pulled up the new number, then the previous one, then the one from a year before. Looked at me. Said "whatever you're doing, keep doing it."
